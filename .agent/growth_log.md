## Lessons Learned & Preferences
1. **Compact & Expressive Front-Panel Design**: Jimmy prefers tight, performance-focused, glassmorphic UI elements over generic placeholders. Side-by-side directories and double-row segmented buttons fit his vision perfectly.
2. **Direct Control & Custom Slicing**: Shifting from a classic hard-routed factory synth into an open, customizable user sampler engine. Saving banks locally (Float32Array channels) rather than using cloud assets matches his cost-effective offline-first style.
3. **Prompt-driven Workflows**: Replacing numbers-only selection prompts with smart, human-readable labels (like `A1-A8` and `B1-B8`) and using clean browser-default confirmations ensures a swift, non-distracting workflow.
4. **Resampler-Style Warp vs Time-Stretching**: Jimmy prefers resampler-style pitch-speed linked loop matching (similar to classic Akai MPC tape-style warping) over complex constant-pitch granular time-stretching, allowing organic speed sweeps and pitch glides that lock instantly to the BPM grid.
5. **Instant Tempo Calculation (FIT BPM)**: Jimmy prefers quick, automated sync utilities (like fitting the master BPM directly to a loop's original duration over a specified beat length) so he can easily determine the native tempo of any sample loop and lock the workstation to it, eliminating tedious manual matching.
6. **Ring-Matching Performance Colors & Custom FX Routing**: Jimmy wants absolute visual synchronization across the performance interface. Binding the performance pad colors to dynamically match their concentric platter tracks and orbiting playheads (and equipping them with right-click context popovers for direct Web Audio routing and stereo panning) creates a highly cohesive, tactile, and expressive live instrument.
7. **Bypassed Audio Routing & Quick Reverse Toggles**: Adding direct, granular bypass routing (`routeToXyPad`) and real-time reverse playback toggles on the performance pads enables lightning-fast live performance manipulations. Jimmy values seamless synchronization where editing a setting in one view (e.g., performance pads) instantly cascades to the audio engine, database, and secondary editor workspaces.
8. **Dedicated Channel EQ Knobs & Transport Layout**: Jimmy prefers rotary knobs for HI, MID, and LOW EQ sculpting in the central mixer column rather than vertical faders. Mapping these knobs to real biquad filter nodes per voice (offering -26dB deep cuts/kills and +6dB boosts) provides professional, standard DJ mixer audio quality. Moving transport controls (Sync, Cue, Play/Pause) directly under the 2x4 pads grid keeps performance controls grouped logically for fast, symmetrical deck execution.
9. **Jitter-Free Metronome & Beat-Synced Recording**: Jimmy values clock stability and precise loops. Increasing lookahead safety buffers (to 250ms) and adding out-of-sync recovery ensures the metronome remains rock-solid under heavy CPU load (e.g. voice triggers and resampling callbacks). Snapping manual recordings to metronome beat boundaries guarantees that resampled loops loop perfectly with zero gaps, making the click track a functional helper rather than just a guide.
10. **Loose Timing & Swing**: Jimmy prefers loose timing and organic swing for finger-drumming on sample slices. Only applying note quantization during active MIDI performance recording ensures that live playing/resampling remains responsive, expressive, and unconstrained by rigid grids.
11. **Comprehensive Slot Schema Alignment**: When migrating slot schemas (from legacy `s01` patterns to `a01`/`b01` partitioned bank formats), Jimmy values comprehensive alignment. Making sure that all fallback constants (such as program loading default waves, arpeggiator slice routes, and database deletion prompts) are systematically updated ensures a robust, bug-free codebase without legacy remnants.
12. **High-Precision Thread-Isolated Clocking**: For high-frequency events (metronome clicks, arpeggio notes, and sequencer playback), Jimmy values microsecond-level timing accuracy. Moving the scheduling logic from main-thread `setInterval`/`setTimeout` intervals directly into an inline AudioWorklet running on the Web Audio render thread completely decouples timing stability from UI workload, heavy voice triggering, and resample recording.
13. **Gamified Rhythm-Game Visuals (Vertical Sequence Highways)**: Jimmy loves highly interactive, visual, and performance-oriented visualizers—like professional vertical scrolling sequencer lanes sitting directly below the Sync, Cue, and Play transport rows of each deck—over traditional horizontal scrolling grids. Having visual events flow towards the playhead target lines makes live sequencing feel alive and responsive.
14. **Tactile Seek Navigation & Direct Beat Readouts**: Jimmy values immediate navigation controls (like quick `-4b`, `+4b`, and `RST` buttons) and direct, zero-overhead digital timers showing the playbeat at 60fps, allowing him to navigate long sequences cleanly and perform live drops/rewinds.
15. **Standard DAW Transport & Dedicated Stop Function**: Jimmy prefers a standard tape-style DAW transport control strip—Rewind (`⏪`), Stop (`■ Stop`), Play/Pause (`▶ Play` / `⏸ Pause`), Forward (`⏩`), and Record (`● Record`)—placed in the bottom digital sequencer bar. Having a dedicated Stop button that silences active voices and resets the playhead back to `0.0` beats is critical for standard playback workflows.
16. **Live Overdub (DUB) Recording Mode**: Jimmy prefers overdub recording (DUB) that layers new triggers onto existing sequences instead of wiping them. Supporting live punch-ins (engaging overdub during playback) and seamless transitions back to playback when exiting overdub is essential for natural, uninterrupted performance workflows.
17. **Deck Solo Mode (Choke Groups)**: Jimmy prefers having tactile Solo toggles instead of redundant Sync buttons under the DJ Decks. This enables exclusive pad triggering (choke groups) per deck, allowing only one sample or slice to play at a time for extremely clean transitions and finger-drumming cuts.
18. **Always-Audible Waveform Previews**: When programming boundaries in the Sample Editor's slice grid, Jimmy prefers that clicking slice buttons plays them back at full volume, bypassing the synth engine's volume sliders, faders, balance, and filter cutoff states, ensuring that transients are always audible during editing.
19. **Web Audio Memory Leak & Resource Audit**: Jimmy values hardware resource safety. In single-page app environments (like his portfolio site), components are mounted/unmounted frequently. Explicitly calling `.disconnect()` on all temporary voice nodes (OSCs, gains, filters, panners) when notes finish decay, and calling `audioCtx.close()` to release system hardware audio channels on component unmount, prevents cumulative browser resource exhaustion and keeps the app running fast.
20. **Metronome 1-Bar Count-in & Quantization Grid Selector**: Jimmy prefers a professional, quantized grid when recording sequences to get tight loops, and wants an armed metronome count-in (1 bar / 4 ticks) with a visible digital readout before recording/overdub starts so he can prepare his timing.
21. **Uncluttered Performance Decks**: Jimmy values clean and focused interface layouts. Removing redundant pitch sliders, key locks, and beat multiplication buttons that clutter the performance deck visual space allows the main performance visual elements (turntables, pads, vertical highways, and the central mixer) to stand out and keep his focus locked during play.
22. **Centering Sequencers & 400px Vertical Highways**: Jimmy prefers vertical sequencers to sit directly below each deck (below sync, cue, play), rather than next to them. Increasing the height of the highways to `400px` and adding Tronesque cyan horizontal grid lines with precise left/right monospace bar/beat labels (e.g. 'BAR 1', '1.2', etc.) scrolling in real-time provides high visual density and professional DAW-like timeline tracking.
23. **High-Precision Decimal BPM support**: Jimmy values flexibility in tempo settings, wanting the master BPM knob, slider, text input, tap-tempo, and fit-bpm syncing to support 0.1 decimal BPM precision (e.g. 120.4 BPM). Decoupling calculations from integers and handling float values natively in the AudioWorklet and main thread updates ensures that tempo changes remain precise and sample-accurate.
24. **Sequencer Transport Controls on both DJ Decks**: Placing dedicated, compact tape-style transport controls (⏪, ■ Stop, ▶ Play / ⏸ Pause, ⏩, ● Rec) directly above the vertical sequencer highways of both DJ Decks allows immediate performance control (seeking, stopping, and recording) on the fly, without needing to scroll down or look away from the active deck.
25. **DJ Slip/Flux Mode (Audible Gate)**: Jimmy prefers DJ Slip/Flux mode on performance pads to act as an audible gate rather than restarting the sample from the beginning or offset. Muting the output gain on pad release (gain to 0 over 15ms) and unmuting on pad press (gain to current fader/crossfader volume over 15ms) keeps the sample voice running silently in the background in phase, allowing smooth, organic live performance drops.
26. **Right-Click Routing Without Interrupting Playback**: Jimmy prefers that right-clicking a performance pad to open its routing context menu does not trigger or interrupt the pad's playback. Restricting pad triggers (`onMouseDown`/`onMouseUp`) exclusively to left-clicks (`e.button === 0`) and checking the active button state (`e.buttons & 1`) in hover/leave handlers (`handlePadGridMouseLeave`) ensures that the pad continues to play uninterrupted when opening, closing, or navigating the popover routing overlay.
27. **Decoupling High-Frequency DOM Animations from React Reconciliation**: To prevent animation skips, jitters, or style resets during parameter adjustments (like moving faders or clicking buttons), high-frequency animated elements (like the concentric vinyl playhead rings and glowing dots) must be completely decoupled from React's virtual DOM reconciliation. Simply omitting style props in JSX is insufficient because React's diffing engine will clear DOM style attributes that are absent in JSX during reconciliation. Creating and appending these animated elements manually as HTML `div`s inside a mount `useEffect` (attaching them to static absolute wrapper `div`s) ensures React is completely unaware of them, preventing style clears. Adding `will-change: transform`, `translate3d(0, 0, 0)`, and nesting the dots inside the rotating tracks forces hardware GPU compositing, halving the number of active DOM writes and ensuring lag-free, butter-smooth 60fps animations.
28. **Lock-Free Atomic Threading & GPU translation layers**: High-frequency performance pad triggers and sequence playback timing can be fully isolated from main-thread CPU workloads by utilizing a lock-free `SharedArrayBuffer` ring buffer mapped to the AudioWorklet render thread. Utilizing `Atomics` load/store operations ensures microsecond-accurate triggers without triggering UI delays. Additionally, promoting scrolling timeline views (like the vertical sequence highways) to GPU compositing layers via `translate3d(0, y, 0)` and `will-change: transform`, combined with memoizing their JSX representation and updating highlight targets directly in the DOM, guarantees butter-smooth 60fps sequencing completely independent of React state updates or parameter adjustments.
29. **Decoupling typing from numeric state (Controlled Input decoupling)**: Decoupling text inputs for numeric params (like master tempo) using a local display string state (`tempoInputVal`) instead of binding them directly to the state object. This ensures that partially typed or deleted strings (such as an empty string when hitting Delete) do not pollute the parameter object, preventing downstream arithmetic or method errors (like calling `.toFixed` on a string or dividing by `NaN`) from crashing React and blanking the screen. Normalization is deferred to `onBlur` with a `useEffect` to sync external updates.
30. **Dual Typed Array Views for SharedArrayBuffer**: `Atomics` operations (like `Atomics.load` and `Atomics.store`) throw `TypeError` if executed on non-integer typed arrays (like `Float32Array`). Instantiating dual views (an `Int32Array` view for index counters/flags and a `Float32Array` view for timestamps/BPM/values) on both the main thread and in the AudioWorklet resolves this limitation. Ensuring the main thread only uses the SAB path when the worklet node is actually initialized and running guarantees that events fallback gracefully to main-thread DSP during startup instead of getting lost in a silent buffer.
31. **Equal-Power FX Crossfading and Headroom Management (Gain Staging)**: Linear dry/wet crossfading (`1.0 - mix` and `mix`) causes a 3dB volume drop at the 50% midpoint, thinning out the sound. Implementing constant equal-power crossfading (`cos(mix)` and `sin(mix)`) preserves overall acoustic power across the entire sweep. Storing a custom `wetScale` inside the mix node allows applying custom headroom scaling for naturally high-volume or high-feedback effects (0.7 for Overdrive, 0.8 for Flanger, 0.9 for Rotor Speaker, 0.85 for Ring Modulator) when adjusting dry/wet parameters. Staging the master delay return permanently to 0.85 avoids bus clipping. Additionally, routing combined master send gains as `Math.max(sendA, sendB)` instead of averaging them prevents inactive channels from attenuating the active channel's FX sends.
32. **Unified Direct Console Strips and Algorithmic FX Customization**: Jimmy prefers direct, physical console layouts that expose all synth parameters at once, avoiding menus or toggles. Moving the filter/envelope (Select A) and assignable/level (Select B) knobs side-by-side at the bottom of the central Touchview panel provides immediate physical access. In the vacant left-panel space, implementing a dedicated Reverb Unit with direct knobs for Mix, Decay, and Pre-Delay, a High-Cut filter slider, and a Reverb Freeze latch switch, combined with dynamic mathematical impulse response generation (Plate, Long, Short, Hall, Reverse), matches his desire for a premium, highly tactile hardware workflow.
33. **Sequencer Highway Height Tuning**: Decoupling vertical coordinate rendering from container bounds. If vertical sequencer highways are styled with a height that exceeds the layout height budget (e.g. 400px), they overflow the turntable deck columns and get clipped at the bottom, hiding the playhead line, target circles, and alphanumeric labels. Setting the CSS height of the highway container (e.g. `.vertical-highway`) to match the JS playback calculations exactly (170px) guarantees that all markers and lanes remain perfectly visible and aligned inside the performance layout without clipping.
34. **Void-Punching Sequencer Grid Aesthetic**: Visualizing note steps as "cutouts" rather than solid blocks over scrolling layouts. Designing vertical sequencer highways with a solid pitch-black background overlaid with solid glowing Tron-cyan vertical and horizontal lines, and rendering note pills with a matching solid black background and intense inner/outer neon glows of their specific pad colors, creates a striking visual illusion of looking into a glowing void cut into the grid. Coordinating z-indexes (vertical static lines at z-index 1, horizontal lines at z-index 1, pills and active lanes at z-index 2) ensures that the pills completely cover the grid lines underneath, completing the visual cutout illusion without requiring heavy or complex CSS masks. Attaching DOM-managed linear-gradient lane overlays provides responsive bottom-up illumination when pads are active.
35. **Beat-Locked Platter Playheads and Tempo-Invariant Sync**: When sweeping the master BPM/tempo, absolute wall-clock elapsed timers (`elapsed = currentTime - startTime`) cause playhead visual rings and orbiting dots to jump, drift, or lose their start-of-beat alignment due to playback rate changes. Binding visual playhead progress directly to the sequencer's `currentBeat - voice.triggerBeat` makes the visual phase invariant to tempo changes, keeping platters perfectly locked and starting exactly at 12 o'clock (0 degrees) when triggered.
36. **Live Sampler Voice Parameter Syncing**: To ensure performance pads and sampler voices feel 'live' and responsive, parameter changes (FX sends, FX target type, panning, tuning, XY routing) must be applied immediately to active playing voices. Always instantiating a dedicated `sendGainNode` on setup, even if the send level is initially zero, and connecting/routing it dynamically when parameters change via active voice lookups, provides smooth real-time control without requiring pad triggers to be stopped and restarted.
37. **Anti-Zipper Pitch Glides and High-Resolution Tuning**: Modifying standard buffer source `playbackRate` instantly using `setValueAtTime` creates audibly rough zipper noise, clicks, and stepping artifacts during manual sweeps (tuning slider, pitch fader) or master tempo BPM adjustments. Replacing this with a `linearRampToValueAtTime` over a tight 35ms window, combined with upgrading the tuning parameter input resolution to a 0.1-semitone step size parsed as a float, provides butter-smooth analog-style pitch glides that sweep seamlessly without stepping.
